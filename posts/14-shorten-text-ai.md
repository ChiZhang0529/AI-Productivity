# How to Shorten Long Text with AI

You wrote 1,500 words. Your editor wants 800. Or your summary needs to fit a slide. Or your email is three paragraphs and should be one. AI excels at compression—condensing without losing the signal.

## Why Shortening Is Harder Than Expanding

Anyone can add words. Padding is easy. Removing requires judgment: what's essential? What can the reader survive without? AI can do the mechanical work, but you need to guide what stays.

## The Three Levels of Compression

### Light Compression (80% → 60% of original)

Keep everything, just tighten:

- Cut adverbs (usually, really, very, quite)
- Remove redundant phrases ("in order to" → "to")
- Combine related sentences
- Delete obvious explanations

AI does this well: "Shorten this text by 25% while keeping all key points."

### Medium Compression (60% → 40%)

This requires choices:

- Identify the core message
- Keep supporting evidence that directly proves the point
- Delete tangents, examples, and elaboration
- Reformulate into tighter structure

Prompt: "Reduce this to its essential points. Remove examples unless they're critical. Keep the conclusion intact."

### Heavy Compression (40% → 20%)

Extreme cuts for strict limits—a tweet becomes a headline, a page becomes a sentence.

This requires understanding what matters. Prompt AI, but verify: "Is this still accurate? Did anything important get lost?"

## Step-by-Step: Cutting a Blog Post Down

Let's say you wrote a 1,200-word first draft and need 700 words.

### Step 1: Find Your Core

Ask AI: "What is this article really about in one sentence?"

If you can't answer that, the piece has no center. Cutting without a center just produces shorter nonsense.

### Step 2: Identify Sections to Cut

Prompt: "Read this article and suggest which sections could be removed entirely without losing the main argument."

Often you'll find: an unnecessary intro, a tangential example, or a conclusion that restates rather than synthesizes.

### Step 3: Tighten Each Section

For each remaining section:

> "Take this paragraph and make it half as long. Keep the key claim, remove the explanation."

### Step 4: Check Flow

After compression, text often feels choppy. Read it aloud and ask: "Does this still sound like one piece of writing, or did I just chop it into fragments?"

### Step 5: Restore One Good Line

Compression kills voice. After cutting, add one human touch back—something specific, unexpected, or personal that gives readers a reason to continue.

## A Real Example

A newsletter writer I know needed to cut an article from 1,800 to 900 words under deadline pressure. She started hacking away randomly, but each revision felt worse. The piece became both shorter and less coherent.

She switched tactics: instead of cutting randomly, she asked AI to identify the "three sentences without which this article would lose its core argument."

Those three sentences stayed. Everything else became candidate material. She cut 50% of the text, but the structure remained intact because she protected the backbone.

Her test: "If someone only read these three sentences, would they get the point?" When yes, she knew she'd preserved the essential.

## Useful Prompts for Shortening

### General compression:
> "Reduce this text by 30%. Keep all key points and the conclusion."

### For bullet points:
> "Convert these paragraphs into bullet points. Keep only the most important items."

### For executive summaries:
> "Write a 100-word executive summary of this article. Focus on what decision-makers need to know."

### For emails:
> "Rewrite this email in half the words without losing urgency or any requested action."

### For slide notes:
> "Turn this text into speaker notes for a 5-minute presentation. Keep one key point per slide."

## Common Mistakes

**Mistake:** Compressing before understanding what matters. You'll cut the wrong things.

**Better:** Read once for comprehension, identify the core, then compress.

**Mistake:** Removing all nuance. Sometimes a qualification matters.

**Better:** After AI compresses, ask: "What did this lose?"

**Mistake:** Treating compression as permanent. Sometimes you need the full version later.

**Better:** Keep the original. Compressed versions are outputs, not replacements.

## When Not to Compress

Some content shouldn't be shortened:

- Technical explanations where precision matters more than brevity
- Emotional content where the length is part of the impact
- Stories where pacing requires space

Not everything needs to be a tweet.
