## The Essential Gear List

### Shelter & Sleep

#### A Quality Sleeping Bag

This is the one item you should not cheap out on — but that doesn't mean you have to spend $200.

Look for a **mummy-style sleeping bag rated to at least 30°F (-1°C)** if you're camping in spring or fall. For summer-only trips, a 50°F (10°C) rating works fine.

**What actually works:**

- **Coleman North Rim** (~$35-45) — Surprisingly warm for the price. The hollow-fiber fill does its job down to around 35°F. Zips smoothly, rectangular enough to toss and turn.

- **Amazon Basics Rectangular** (~$25-40) — Not the most compact, but comfortable and machine-washable. Good for car camping where weight doesn't matter.

**My tip:** Always test your sleeping bag at home before your trip. Sleep in it for one night in your backyard. You'll figure out if you need an extra blanket or if the zipper annoys you.

#### A Foam Sleeping Pad

A sleeping bag on the ground will steal your body heat fast — even in summer. A foam pad adds insulation and cushion.

**What actually works:**

- **Therm-a-Rest Z Lite Sol** (~$40-50) — Yes, it's at the edge of the budget. But this thing lasts for years, doubles as sitting padding, and weighs under a pound. If you're doing any backpacking later, this pad grows with you.

- **Foam sleeping pads from Outoo or WiseHawk** (~$15-25) — Thick, ridged foam that works great for car camping. Bulkier but half the price.

### Cooking & Food

#### A Portable Camping Stove

Forget fancy jet boil systems when you're starting. A simple **canister stove or liquid fuel stove** does the job at a fraction of the cost.

**What actually works:**

- **Snow Peak GigaPower 2.0** (~$25-35) — Ultralight, simple to use, boils 500ml of water in under 3 minutes. I've had mine for 6 years.

- **Coleman PerfectFlow** (~$20-30) — If you want something more stable with a windscreen, this is reliable. Uses small propane canisters.

**Practical example:** On my first solo trip, I brought the Snow Peak. Dinner was instant noodles and a gas canister that cost $3 total. That's camping on a budget without suffering.

#### A Simple Pot and Pan Set

You don't need a full cookset. One **pot and one pan** covers 90% of meals.

**What actually works:**

- **Stanley Adventure Pot Set** (~$25-35) — Two pots with lids, one pan, folds flat. Stacks neatly.

- **Single aluminum pot with lid** (~$10-15 from any outdoor store) — Works fine for boiling water, soups, and simple meals.

### Lighting & Power

#### A Headlamp

Forget holding a flashlight. A headlamp keeps your hands free for cooking, setting up tents, or walking to the bathroom at night.

**What actually works:**

- **Petzl Tikkina** (~$25-30) — Simple, reliable, 150 lumens. Three AAA batteries last for dozens of hours on low mode.

- **Black Diamond Spot** (~$40) — If you can stretch to $50, this is brighter, waterproof, and has a red light mode to preserve night vision.

**My tip:** Bring extra batteries. Always. The first time your headlamp dies at 2 AM, you'll remember this advice.

### Tools & Safety

#### A Multi-Tool or Knife

You need something to cut rope, open packaging, and handle small repairs.

**What actually works:**

- **Victorinox Swiss Army Classic** (~$25-30) — Small, light, has scissors, knife, tweezers, and more. Fits on a keychain.

- **Gerber Paraframe** (~$20-25) — Simple folding knife with a belt clip. Opens with one hand.

#### A Basic First Aid Kit

You don't need a trauma kit for a weekend trip. A **personal first aid kit with basics** is enough.

Look for kits that include:

- Adhesive bandages (various sizes)
- Antiseptic wipes
- Pain relievers
- Moleskin or blister pads
- Medical tape

**What actually works:**

- **Adventure Medical Kits Personal Kit** (~$20-30) — Compact, well-organized, covers essentials.

- **DIY kit** (~$10-15) — Buy a small waterproof pouch and fill it with basics from a pharmacy. Sometimes cheaper and more personalized.

## Gear I Don't Recommend for Beginners

Before you start filling your cart, here's what **not** to buy when starting out:

- **Expensive tents** — Start with something basic. You might discover you hate camping. A $300 tent shouldn't be your test.
- **Packed "starter kits"** — These usually include low-quality items you won't use. Pick and choose individual pieces instead.
- **Hiking boots over $50** — Good boots matter, but start with what you already own. Sneakers work fine for most beginner trails.
